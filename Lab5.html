In the above DataFrame, we can see the maximum, minimum, and mean values for each of the columns. Before we make plots, we will discuss how the data was taken and what the features of the data mean.

The paper used as a reference in this lab is Identification of boosted Higgs bosons decaying into b-quark pairs with the ATLAS detector at 13 TeV. The data is simulated. There are 100k "jets" and the filed label higgs is the signals and the file labeled ccd is the background dataset.

We are looking at LHC data simulated by Alex Schuy. As we saw above the samples represent a binary classification problem, where we see two different datasets/classes. The first one being jets from Higgs decay and the second one is jets from the QCD background. The features/columns which we explore earlier are kinematic data from the jets and energy correlators. The construction of efficient discriminating observables is typically guided by Monte Carlo simulations. Referencing the paper above, we can go more in depth on how the data was acquired / simulated. We are looking at proton-proton (pp) collision runs and correspond to a total integrated luminosity of 36.1 $fb^{-1}$ at $\sqrt{s}$ = 13 TeV. The paper writes that "several Monte Carlo (MC) simulated event samples were used for the optimisation of the Higgs boson tagger, estimation of its performance, and the comparisons between data and simulation." The Higgs bosons are produced with large transverse momentum (pT) and decaying to a bottom quark-antiquark pair. The problem lies with the fact that due to large amounts of QCD background containmination, a direct 5-sigma observation of this Higgs channel is not accomplished as of yet. The purpose, then, is to enhance the discovery sensitivity by optimizing event selection.


We can now describe what the kinematic features are.

pt: Transverse (x-y plane) momentum
eta: Pseudorapidity
phi: Azimuthal Angle
mass: Invariant Mass
ee2: 2-point ECF ratio
ee3: 3-point ECF ratio
d2: 3-to-2-point ECF ratio
angularity: angularity

With the help of my partner, the last few variables are:
t1: "N-subjettiness measures the degree to which radiation within a jet is aligned along N candidate subjet axes"
t2: 2-subjettiness, for any given particle, we now have two axes instead of one
t3: 3-subjettiness, the mean will be even moreso reduced by the addition of an extra axis compared to t2.
t21: This is the ratio of subjettinesses t2 / t1 for a given jet.
t32: This is the ratio for a given jet of its variables t3 / t2.
KDeltaR:
